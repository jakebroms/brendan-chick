Development

Athlete Development

At Mercer, Brendan has been responsible for developing athletes across multiple Division I sports while continuing to assist with Football Strength & Conditioning.

The biggest lesson from leading teams is that training has to work in the real world. Athletes have practice, competition, travel, soreness, fatigue, academic stress, and different training histories. A good program has to create progress while fitting the demands of the season.

With Men’s Soccer, 90% of athletes PR’d bench press within 10 weeks between baseline and follow-up testing. With Women’s Lacrosse, training helped improve lower-body strength qualities related to ACL injury-risk reduction during a three-week block while the team prepared through a Southern Conference championship push.

Those experiences have made Brendan a better coach because they required ownership. He had to program, coach, adjust, communicate, and produce results across different sports and different competitive demands.

That responsibility is preparing him for his long-term goal: contributing to a high-level football strength and conditioning staff.

Philosophy

Football Strength & Conditioning Philosophy

Brendan’s long-term goal is to work in football strength and conditioning at the highest level.

His coaching philosophy has been shaped by Coach Adam Smotherman, Buddy Morris, Dave Tate, James “The Thinker” Smith, Dan Pfaff, Joe Kenn, and Charlie Francis. Brendan has studied their work because they represent the parts of the profession he cares about most: football preparation, strength development, speed, stress management, organization, and high standards.

Brendan does not believe in copying one system. He believes in studying deeply, applying practically, and adjusting to the athletes and program in front of him.

Football players need to be strong, fast, powerful, durable, and confident. They need training that prepares them for the demands of the game, not just training that looks good on paper. The weight room matters, but the goal is not to win the weight room. The goal is to help athletes play better football.

Brendan believes in simple, hard, well-coached training. He believes in being early, being prepared, coaching with energy, and earning trust through consistency. He believes the strength coach should serve the head coach’s vision, support the staff, and help raise the standard of the entire organization.

Brendan is looking for the right football program because this is the environment he wants to pour himself into.
